Re-Design

Exhibition

Everyday Life in the 21st Century

“Re-Design: The Daily Products of the 21st Century” is a 2000 exhibition curated by Kenya Hara, which invites viewers to reconsider the overlooked objects of everyday life through the lens of design. Items such as toothpaste, egg cartons, fire extinguishers, and face masks things so familiar they’re often ignored were “re-designed” by various designers. Through this, the exhibition demonstrates that design is not about decoration, but about reconstructing meaning and transforming the user experience. It visually embodies Hara’s belief that “design is a medium for messages,” and explores how function, sensation, and social value can coexist within the realm of design.

Through the Redesign exhibition, Kenya Hara aimed to show that design is not just about making things new, but about rediscovering essence and removing the unnecessary to bring deeper meaning and value to life. He emphasized that design holds the power to create a sustainable future based on respect and reflection, not just change and innovation.

The square-shaped tissue roll in Kenya Hara’s Re-Design exhibition is an eco-friendly product that eliminates the inner paper core and adopts a square form to reduce waste and resource consumption. By rethinking the familiar round shape, it challenges what we take for granted in everyday objects, conveying the aesthetic of “Emptiness” and promoting a message of sustainability.

"Design is not about

creating something new,

but about rediscovering

the meaning of what already exists."

Kenya Hara’s statement, “Design is not about creating something new, but about rediscovering the meaning of what already exists,” emphasizes that design is not merely the act of inventing entirely new things. Instead, it is a process of uncovering hidden value within the objects and phenomena that already surround us, and reinterpreting them in a way that brings their meaning to light. He sees design as an act of “rediscovery” rather than “creation,” and believes that true design lies in the sensitivity and insight to reveal depth and beauty in the ordinary elements of everyday life.
