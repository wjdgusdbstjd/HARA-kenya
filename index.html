原硏哉

Born in 1958, in Okayama City, Okayama Prefecture, Japan

Kenya Hara is a renowned Japanese graphic designer, branding director, and exhibition curator. As the art director of MUJI, he redefined the brand’s identity through the core concepts of “emptiness,” “white space,” and “sensation,” continuously interpreting traditional Japanese aesthetics in a contemporary context. Rather than viewing design as a purely visual outcome, he understands it as a medium of experience that influences human perception and sensation—guiding the audience not toward filling space, but toward approaching the essence through the act of emptying.


His work spans across product design, spatial design, exhibition planning, and editorial design, constantly crossing disciplinary boundaries to explore how design can connect with society, the environment, and everyday life. Through books such as White, Designing Design, and Ex-formation, he emphasizes that the role of design is not merely to explain, but to help people perceive the invisible. In an age of information overload, his work invites us to reconsider the value of emptiness.

“Design is not something to be seen,

but something to be felt.”

Hara redefines design as something to be felt, not just seen. He empahasizes multisensory experiences, where touch, sound, and space contribute to meaning. His HAPTIC exhibition (2004) explored how design can awaken non-visual senses.

“White contains all colors,

and white space holds all possibilities.”

For Kenya Hara, white is not just a color it’s a space filled with possibility. White space is not “empty” but a place where perception and contemplation dwell. It serves as a medium of silence and pause, inviting viewers to engage beyond visuals.

“I believe that in design,

subtracting is more important

than adding.”

Hara believes that removing is more important than adding. “Emptiness” in his design is not mere deletion but an act of lea ving only the essential. This philosophy underpins his work with MUJI, where minimalism meets meaning.
